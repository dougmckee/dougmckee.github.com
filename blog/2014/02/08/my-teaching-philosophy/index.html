My Teaching Philosophy

Feb 8th, 2014

In the past five years, I have done a lot of teaching. In total, I’ve taught 25 classes covering many different formats for several different disciplines ranging from economics to public health to medicine. Reflecting on these experiences, I would characterize my approach to teaching as having three core parts. First, students must actively engage in the learning process inside and outside the classroom. Second, there is incredible value in building a high level of trust with students. Third, in addition to teaching facts and knowledge, I teach and nurture creativity.

I find that students learn and retain far more when they are actively involved in the process. I encourage this engagement by teaching classes that are a series of short (15-20 minute) lectures, discussions, and group activities. I motivate topics with questions that the students care about personally, intellectually, and socially, and I try to build connections between what they already know and the new material I teach. In this way, I make teaching personal so that each student feels individually invested in the class. I make learning active by creating opportunities for individuals to express their opinions and concerns. In seminars, I often have students work in small groups. In lectures I work hard to find examples that my students will find interesting and intersperse many “practice breaks” in which students work on practicing the skills I have just taught while I walk around and engage with individuals.

One of the best ways to come up with questions and topics that students care about is to get to know students as people. Building a level of trust with my students pays off in many ways. It allows me to recover from the inevitable mistakes all teachers make in front of the class. It means students stay engaged even if they don’t understand a new idea right away, and it means they will tell me when they don’t understand instead of simply tuning out. To build this level of trust, I try to learn their names quickly, talk to students after class and in office hours, and find out what motivates them individually. I treat my undergraduates as adults, and I give them honest constructive feedback on their work. I expect them to work hard, and in return I work hard.

In addition to teaching the substantive content of a course, I work hard to teach creativity. Many students come to my classes eager to learn facts and methods, but I try to show them how to ask smart questions and think creatively about how to answer these questions. While most economics classes show and analyze formal economic models, I require my students to write down new models that formalize their own ideas. I try to inspire them to be intellectually ambitious and reward this ambition by investing extra time to guide it or bring order to their thoughts.
